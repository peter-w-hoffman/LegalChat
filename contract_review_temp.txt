Financing Contingency (Clause 4.A)  \nClause text (excerpt):  \n  “The Buyer’s obligations herein are contingent on the Buyer’s obtaining financing … must present … a binding commitment … within ___ days … terms … acceptable to and approved by the Buyer who shall not unreasonably withhold such approval.  In the event the Buyer fails to obtain financing within the time allotted, this Agreement shall automatically terminate and all funds paid by the Buyer shall be returned … after deducting all reasonable costs incurred by the Seller in good faith in relation to this Agreement.”  \n\nSimilar precedent language:  \n  –  “[I]n order to help finance … the BUYER shall apply for … a mortgage loan of $399,000 … If despite the BUYER’s diligent efforts a commitment for such a loan cannot be obtained on or before [deadline], the BUYER may terminate this agreement by written notice … any payments made … shall be forthwith refunded.”    \n  –  “If [the buyer] gives timely notice … that he has been unable to obtain financing … the agreement becomes null and void … If he does not communicate his intention to terminate … he runs the risk of losing his deposit and being compelled to perform.”    \n\nWhy those precedents spawned disputes:  \n  In Chin v. Moraine Homes, LLC, the court faced conflicting loan-type language—“conventional bank” vs. “construction to permanent”—and held that absent clear definition of “diligent efforts” and of what loans were “acceptable,” the parties litigated extrinsic evidence over intent and good-faith diligence .  In Martorella v. Rapp the buyer’s failure to give any notice led the seller to treat the contract as live and to seek the deposit, triggering litigation over whether notice was required .  \n\nWhy the current clause is risky:  \n  •\tNo definition of “binding commitment,” “acceptable terms,” or “reasonable costs.”  \n  •\tNo express requirement that the Buyer notify the Seller before termination, so the Buyer may inadvertently default and forfeit the deposit.  \n  •\tBuyer’s unilateral right to “approve” loan terms subjectively invites dispute.  \n\nProposed redline:  \n  “A. FINANCING CONTINGENCY. Buyer’s obligation to close is conditioned upon Buyer’s obtaining a loan commitment on commercially reasonable terms. Buyer shall, in good faith and with due diligence, apply for financing of the Purchase Price within ___ days of the Effective Date. If Buyer is unable to obtain a written commitment for a mortgage loan on commercially reasonable terms by that deadline, Buyer may terminate by delivering written notice to Seller no later than 5:00 p.m. on the deadline date. Upon such notice this Agreement shall be void and Buyer’s Earnest Money shall be refunded in full, and Seller shall bear no costs except those expressly agreed in writing. Nothing in this provision obligates Buyer to accept financing on terms exceeding market standards for similar properties.”  \n\n2. TITLE CONTINGENCY.  Seller shall deliver at Seller’s expense an owner’s title insurance commitment, showing title free of all exceptions except those listed in Schedule A. If Seller elects not to or fails to cure within that period, Buyer may terminate and receive a full refund of Earnest Money, reimbursement of Buyer’s out-of-pocket title search and insurance premiums (not to exceed $___), and documented closing costs (not to exceed $___).”  \n\n3.  Default and Liquidated Damages (Clause 8)  \nClause text (excerpt):  \n  “In the event that the Buyer defaults, the Buyer shall forfeit the Earnest Money as liquidated damages in favor of the Seller, who shall have no other remedy in such an event.”  \n\nSimilar precedent language:  \n  –  “If the Buyer shall fail to fulfill the Buyer's agreement … all deposits made hereunder by the Buyer shall be retained by the Seller as liquidated damages, as Seller’s sole and exclusive remedy.”    \n  –  “A deposit equal to five percent of the purchase price was a reasonable forecast of damages in the event of breach.”    \n  –  “The language … is clear and unambiguous, and the $30,000 deposit was intended as liquidated damages.”    \n\nWhy those precedents spawned disputes:  \n  In NRT New England, Inc. v. Moncure the court enforced a 5%-of-price deposit as reasonable under Kelly v. Marx because “potential damages were difficult to determine” and the amount was not “grossly disproportionate” .  In Barry v. Thayer the seller kept a large deposit after buyer canceled, and the buyer unsuccessfully argued it was a penalty; the court held the plain language controlled .  \n\nWhy the current clause is risky:  \n  •\tIt does not state the deposit as a fixed percentage of Purchase Price or cap it—leaving open challenges that it may be punitive.  \n  •\tSeller has “no other remedy,” which may conflict with Buyer’s right to specific performance.  \n\nProposed redline:  \n  “DEFAULT.  If Buyer fails to close except as permitted hereunder, Seller’s sole remedy shall be to retain as liquidated damages the deposit equal to five percent (5%) of the Purchase Price, which the parties agree constitutes a reasonable estimate of Seller’s anticipated damages.  This remedy is exclusive and in lieu of all other remedies at law or in equity.”  \n\n4.  Arbitration (Clause 10.B)  \nClause text (excerpt):  \n  “In the event … disputes remain … the Parties agree to submit such unresolved dispute to binding arbitration … tribunal of three arbitrators … third appointed by the two … oral hearings … language English … tribunal … may order reasonable discovery … may not award any punitive or exemplary damages whatsoever … award … final and binding … each Party bear its own legal fees, save to the extent the tribunal orders otherwise.”  \n\nSimilar precedent language:  \n  –  “[A]ny dispute or claim arising out of or relating to the Agreement … shall be submitted to binding arbitration by REBA Dispute Resolution …” (in a Rider A that was not signed and thus held unenforceable)   \n  –  “Where a contract contains an arbitration clause, there is a presumption of arbitrability … [and] the arbitration clause applies broadly to ‘future disputes’”   \n\nWhy those precedents spawned disputes:  \n  In Taylor v. 28 Marsh St. Hingham the court compelled arbitration only after finding the Riders were properly incorporated by reference and signed; here, no administering rules or provider are designated, leaving doubt whether the clause is enforceable or how to initiate proceedings .  In Miller v. The arbitrators shall have the power to grant any relief authorized by this Agreement or applicable law, including specific performance (but excluding punitive damages).  Discovery shall be conducted in accordance with the AAA’s Expedited Procedures.  The award shall be in writing, reasoned, and rendered within ninety (90) days after the hearing’s close.  Judgment on the award may be entered in any court of competent jurisdiction.  The prevailing party shall be entitled to recover its reasonable attorneys’ fees and costs.”  \n\n5. Lenders routinely require the right to assign or collaterally transfer purchase agreements for securitization.  \n\nProposed redline:  \n  “ASSIGNMENT.  Buyer may not assign its rights or obligations under this Agreement without Seller’s prior written consent, which consent shall not be unreasonably withheld or delayed; provided, however, Buyer may assign this Agreement without Seller’s consent to (a) a lender or proposed lender in connection with financing the purchase, (b) an entity controlled by Buyer, or (c) an affiliate of Buyer, so long as the assignor remains liable for performance hereunder and the transferee executes an assumption agreement acceptable to Seller.”  \n\nThese redlines sharpen each clause by defining key terms, specifying processes and deadlines, and narrowing remedies to eliminate the uncertainty that regularly fuels litigation.
